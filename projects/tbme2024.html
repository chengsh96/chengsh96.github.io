Automatic Stub Avoidance for a Powered Prosthetic Leg Over Stairs and Obstacles (TBME 2024)

TBME 2024 Featured Article
Research
TBME 2024 Paper Watch Video

Toe stubbing during swing is a common safety risk for transfemoral prosthesis users, especially on stairs and during obstacle negotiation. This work adds a reactive safety layer on top of continuous locomotion control: the controller predicts insufficient clearance and automatically reshapes swing trajectories to increase toe clearance, while preserving smoothness and user comfort.

The core idea is to treat stub avoidance as a trajectory adjustment problem, not a mode switch: when a risk is detected, the system computes a minimum-jerk corrective trajectory and blends it into the nominal gait to avoid abrupt motion or destabilizing transients. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

What I built

  • Real-time risk estimation: detected toe-clearance risk using prosthesis kinematics (e.g., knee/shank angles) combined with ultrasonic distance measurements to stairs/obstacles. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}
  • Minimum-jerk trajectory reshaping: computed smooth corrective knee trajectories and blended them into the nominal motion to preserve comfort and stability. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}
  • Compatibility with continuous control: integrated the safety layer with the continuous controller so that avoidance behavior is consistent across locomotion modes (walking and stairs), rather than bolted-on as a separate “special case.” :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Results & robustness highlights

  • Large reduction in stub events on stairs (risky stepping): in a “safe-line” protocol designed to induce high stubbing risk, the controller reduced stub rate from ~49% to ~5% overall, with participant reductions of 87.6% and 92.3%. :contentReference[oaicite:7]{index=7}
  • Obstacle-crossing effectiveness: achieved successful obstacle crossings, with remaining scuffs tied to conservative knee-velocity limits that were later tuned (demonstrating practical deployment tradeoffs). :contentReference[oaicite:8]{index=8}
  • Low false-trigger rate across multi-activity circuits: in an offline simulation spanning diverse daily activities (turning, ramps, stairs, sit/stand), the false-positive rate for noticeable extra flexion was 1.23%, and all extra flexions returned to nominal by heel strike. :contentReference[oaicite:9]{index=9}
